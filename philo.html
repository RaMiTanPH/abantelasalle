PHILOSOPHY

Contact Reflection Paper about Environmental Dilemmas in a Philosophical View

The world faces an unprecedented ecological crisis, with climate change and species extinction threatening our survival. Ecological peace or the balance between humanity and the environment is essential for global responsibility and sustainability. Achieving this requires collective action and a shared commitment to diverse beliefs and ideologies.

Overconsumption of natural resources is a major driver of biodiversity loss, fueled by the increasing demands of a growing population. As businesses produce more goods to meet human needs, resource depletion and environmental degradation threaten future life and progress. Land use changes, particularly for large-scale food production, account for 30% of global biodiversity loss, while overexploitation of resources like food, medicine, and lumber contributes another 20%. To address this, a shift toward sustainable consumption and production is essential, emphasizing reduced resource use, recycling, and long-lasting products. By embracing a circular economy, reducing food waste, and implementing innovative policies, we can mitigate biodiversity loss and promote environmental sustainability. Virtue ethics supports this shift by encouraging responsible behavior that aligns with the "golden mean," ensuring we balance resource use with conservation to safeguard the future for coming generations.

Plastic pollution, while offering undeniable benefits in fields like medicine and technology, presents a significant environmental dilemma due to the rapid rise in disposable plastic products. Single-use plastics, which make up around 40% of all plastic production, persist in the environment for hundreds of years despite being used for only a few minutes. This creates a direct conflict with Sustainable Development Goal 12 (SDG 12), which promotes sustainable consumption and production patterns. To mitigate the damage, solutions include reducing plastic production, improving waste management, promoting eco-friendly alternatives like biodegradable materials, and fostering global cooperation. Ethically, consequentialism supports the shift away from single-use plastics by prioritizing long-term environmental outcomes. Through responsible production and conscious consumption, we can align with SDG 12 and minimize the environmental impact while still benefiting from plastics' advantages.

Economic growth is often seen as a key factor in improving living standards and advancing innovation, but it also raises concerns about environmental degradation and inequality. While growth can enhance job creation, infrastructure, and social services, it can also exploit natural resources, worsen ecological damage, and deepen social inequalities. The ethical implications of growth depend on how it is managedâ€”whether it promotes inclusivity and sustainability or exacerbates exploitation and environmental harm. To address these challenges, strategies like sustainable development and inclusive growth are vital, aiming to balance economic expansion with social equity and environmental protection. Virtue ethics, emphasizing values like accountability and justice, offers a framework for guiding growth in a way that benefits both current and future generations.

Carbon dioxide emissions, which account for 79.7% of global greenhouse gases, significantly contribute to global warming, with jeepneys being a major source of emissions in the Philippines. The Public Utility Vehicle (PUV) Modernization Program aims to address this by replacing old, inefficient jeepneys with modern, less polluting alternatives. From a consequentialist perspective, the program is essential to reduce carbon emissions and benefit the environment, but from a deontological standpoint, it is unethical to burden drivers with the high costs of new jeepneys without sufficient financial support. A balanced solution requires government subsidies to make the transition feasible for drivers while ensuring sustainable, low-emission public transportation.

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) is a controversial climate change solution that faces criticism for its potential leakage risks, which could cause severe environmental damage. Ethical concerns arise from the high costs associated with CCS technology and the long-term safety of underground carbon storage, disproportionately affecting poorer nations that lack the resources for such investments. The author argues for prioritizing renewable energy sources like solar and wind as more ethical and effective alternatives, as they generate power without emissions and promote environmental sustainability. Additionally, enhancing natural processes for carbon absorption, such as reforestation, is proposed as a just approach to combat climate change. Ultimately, from a consequentialist perspective, the potential harms of CCS, including ecosystem damage and delayed renewable energy adoption, outweigh its benefits, making it an unethical choice.
